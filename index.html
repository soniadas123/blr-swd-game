What are the dots?

Each dot represents a real-world observation collected in the field.

The locations were recorded using ODK (Open Data Kit), an open-source mobile application used to collect georeferenced field data.

What is ODK?

ODK is an open-source tool for collecting data in the field.

It allows users to record observations along with GPS coordinates, photographs, timestamps, and survey responses—all from a mobile device.

What are Vrishabhavathi, Koramangala, and Hebbal?

Bengaluru's natural terrain is shaped by five major valleys that guide how rainwater flows through the city:

  • Hebbal Valley
  • Koramangala–Challaghatta Valley
  • Vrishabhavathi Valley
  • Arkavathi Valley
  • Suvarnamukhi Valley

This visualisation contains observations collected from three of these valleys:

  • Hebbal Valley
  • Koramangala–Challaghatta Valley
  • Vrishabhavathi Valley

Why visualise locations with timestamps?

A location tells us where something was observed.

A timestamp tells us when it was observed.

Together, they allow us to understand patterns, monitor change, and build evidence for better decisions.

This visualisation is a small experiment in showing how space and time work together.

Why build this?

I wanted to explore whether geospatial concepts could be introduced through interaction rather than explanation.

Instead of starting with technical terms, I wanted to begin with curiosity.

A simple visualisation can lead to bigger questions:

  • How is location recorded?
  • How accurate is GPS?
  • Why does time matter?
  • How is field data collected?
  • Why use open-source tools?
  • What happens to the data after it's collected?

Those questions are the beginning of geospatial science.
